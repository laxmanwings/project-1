AUTONOMOUS DRIVING

  1. Home
  2. AUTONOMOUS DRIVING

Autonomous driving has received a unified definition by the automotive industry and can be best described as “the capability of a car to drive partly or fully by itself, with limited or no human intervention.” According to the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE), taxonomy of vehicle driving automation systems, conditional and high automations are registered in SAE level 3 and SAE level 4 vehicle categories, respectively. Vehicle category SAE level 5 describes fully automated vehicles with driving systems being able to perform the full range of driving functions and modes previously performed by a human driver including steering, acceleration/deceleration, monitoring of the driving environment, and assuming control of all aspects of driving. expected to improve road safety, traffic flow, personal mobility, eco driving, and driver productivity. The variety of driving automation technology in supporting the human driver can be classified toward their level of autonomy (LoA). Each LoA defines the degree to which a vehicle can take over the operation of the dynamic driving task. Based on the international literature, a few taxonomies have been introduced to define distinct degrees of vehicle automation. Out of these, the taxonomy approach developed by SAE (Society of Automotive Engineers), which is outlined in, represents the most recent and comprehensive one where six different levels of vehicle automation about on-road car passenger vehicles are classified. The above levels of increased autonomy differ by the operation of the longitudinal/lateral control and the monitoring of the environment, which can be either controlled by the driver or the on-board driving automation system.

Safety:

Monitoring of the driving environment in order to identify potential problems and emergency situations and automatically take actions in order to secure the passengers’ safety and prevent potential accidents. Safety issues are monitored via the Advanced Driving Assistance Systems (ADAS) and cover features like driving assistance, collision protection, and emergency breaking. The evolution of ADAS technologies is expected to constitute a vital factor in the autonomous driving system with major importance regarding consumer acceptance.

Connectivity:

Connectivity features improve the car’s operation and are closely related with already established telematics and infotainment systems within cars. In the concept of autonomous driving, those features could be either provided as extra (payable) services by the car’s software and/or aftermarket services, or as built-in functions purchased alongside the AV model. Those services could not only include entertainment features and optimal fuel consumption in the concept of the AV (already present in modern vehicles) but also expand into connectivity between different AVs and outside infrastructure in the concept of a synchronized autonomous car fleet.

Autonomous driving systems:

The development of systems that are able to automate the activity of driving is essentially the core of autonomous driving. A complete autonomous driving system can be described as an integration of already established systems of adaptive cruise control, parking assistance, and autopilots into a unified function that uses artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning to adapt its driving behavior based on driver preferences and data from the related safety and connectivity functions.

The majority of these technologies are already developed and included in modern vehicles but currently lack a unified interface to integrate them altogether in SAE level 5 automated vehicle. Currently, Tesla’s Autopilot is the only SAE level 4 autonomous system in the market, with other OEMs already establishing autonomous features in their models but not a related autopilot system. The majority of OEMs, including traditional powerhouses such as Toyota, Volvo, BMW, Ford, and others, are already heavily engaged in investing in autonomous systems in order to secure their dominant market position from new, tech-heavy digital players that dynamically enter the market, such as Waymo, an Alphabet (Google) subsidiary, self-driving car company.
